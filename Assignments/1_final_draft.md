# Memo: Sun Tzu and Strategy from Contrast

### Introduction

Strategy from contrast is the practice of grounding strategic choice in moves that deliberately oppose widespread intuition, conventional wisdom, or the “obvious” consensus - because the competitive edge lies in exploiting precisely those shared assumptions.

Sun Tzu was an ancient Chinese military strategist and philosopher, best known as the traditional author of **The Art of War**. His work is dates back to the 5th century BCE, and exhibits various forms of strategy. In this memo we will be looking at his depictions of "Strategy from contrast", especially from the **Chapter 6: Energy** from **The Art of War**.


# 1. Power of Subdivisions

## Popular intuition:
- Mass wins.
- The army should be as large and as unified a block as possible
- Sheer scale signals power and overwhelms the opponent.

## Contrast:
- Banking on a solid block of army is **at best "incomplete" & at worst "insufficient"** to win a war. 
- Effectiveness comes from **how** force is organized and **alternated**, not only from how much of it exists. There are 2 main types of applying force:

    1. **Cheng (正):** The fixed, visible front — **which fixes the enemy’s attention**. It tends to read as **passive** in the sense of holding a line or role while waiting for the right moment.
    2. **Chi (奇):** The lateral, surprising thrust - **which catches the enemy by surprise**. It is active in the sense of breaking pattern.

- Combining both of them yields **infinite variations** in maneuver which is a strategic logic that contradicts the idea that there is one best formation, which is going all in with "one big giant punch".

### Why this is Strategy from Contrast?
1. Great leaders like Alexander the Great and Napoleon Bonaparte believe in size and unity whereas Sun Tzu privilege structured alternation and role-switching.
2. The counterintuitive claim is that a **smaller, rightly divided** operation, if fused with rhythmic cheng–chi use, can outperform a single rigid mass that the enemy can read once and counter forever.

# 2. Appearance vs. Reality

## Popular intuition:
- In a fight, **show strength**.
- Looking strong deters attack and rallies allies.

## Contrast:
- Deception is the key.
- If we are stronger or comparable in strength, then we should simulate weakness to lure the enemy by overconfidence.
- If we are inferior in strength, then we should simulate strength to buy space and deter premature attack.

### Story Time: Depicting use of Deception:

- The Han emperor wanted to crush the Hsiung-nu and sent spies.
- The nomads, **forewarned**, hid their **able-bodied men and well-fed horses** and let the scouts see only infirm soldiers and emaciated cattle.
- **Every spy** came back urging attack — the obvious inference after looking at the camp.
- **Lou Ching (Counselor to Emperor)** argued the contrastive reading: when two states go to war, each side normally **flaunts** strength. Hence, to see only age and weakness is too convenient** and must be a **ruse**.
- But the emperor **followed consensus**, attacked, and was **surrounded at Po-teng**.

THE GIST: **consensus** trusted the **sign**; **structure** was the opposite of the sign.

### Why this is Strategy from Contrast?
- A comment made by author is that **“In fact, all the enemy’s movements should be determined by the signs that we choose to give him.”** which is again a contrastove statement as we are always trying to control ONLY our movements BUT -- **OUR** dispositions and subdivisions **script** the other side’s inferences — if they take visible weakness, order, or head-count as transparent.
- Ordinary life rewards **congruence** between appearance and reality; but as seen §18 and its stories reward **engineered divergence** — order that wears disorder, courage that wears fear, strength that wears weakness — so that the enemy’s **common-sense optics** become the lever you pull.

# 3. Seeming chaos as disciplined subdivision (§16)

## Popular intuition:
- In battle, show **strength and clarity**.
- Disorder means you are **losing**.
- A jumbled formation (“no clear front or back”) sounds like a **disaster**.

## Contrast:
- Even in noise and chaos, your side can **look** messy but **not actually** be messy.
- Sun Tzu (§16): there can be **seeming disorder** without **real** disorder; your line can look **headless and tail-less** and still **hold** and not collapse.
- Commentator Mei Yao-ch`en spells it out in plain terms: **units and roles are set ahead of time**; **everyone knows the signals**; splitting and rejoining in the fight may **look** wild from the outside, but **orders still run the show** — control is **in the plan**, not in “looking neat.”

### Why this is Strategy from Contrast?
1. Most people read a chaotic field as **loss of control**; Sun Tzu reads it as **flexibility on purpose** — motion that **confuses the enemy** but **does not** confuse your own troops if the drill is solid.
2. People think **one big, tidy block** is “real strength”; Sun Tzu says **smaller, coordinated pieces** that move and signal together can be **harder to predict** and **harder to break**.

# 4. Self-restraint and timing

## Popular intuition:
- When you are under fire, **hit back right away**.
- Waiting looks like **weakness**.
- The “strong” leader shoots **first** and **fastest**.

## Contrast:
- A good decision is compared to a **falcon**: the kill happens in one stroke because the bird **waits** until the distance and moment are right — not because it flaps around early (§13).
- Tu Mu links this to **holding your fire** until it will **actually count** — the opposite of panicking and blasting away.
- The same line of thought (§14): a strong attacker is **decisive** — sharp **because** the strike is **timed**, not because it is random or rushed.

### Story Time: Nelson at Trafalgar (example Tu Mu uses for §13):

- This is **not** from China; it is a story about **Nelson** and the ship **HMS *Victory***.
- The ship took a **heavy beating** and for several **minutes** did **not** answer shot for shot.
- Nelson **waited** until he was **close enough**; then the broadside he fired did **serious damage**.
- The lesson in the commentary: **not shooting back immediately** can be the **smart** move — patience is **measuring distance and timing**, not cowardice.

THE GIST: **Waiting** can be **how** you land a blow that matters; **speed** without timing wastes force.

### Why this is Strategy from Contrast?
1. Movies and gut instinct say **action = strength**; this part of the Energy chapter says **well-timed hold** (or **held fire**) can be **stronger agency** than firing first.
2. “Strike back instantly” is the default heroic picture; **strike when it will actually work** is the contrast Sun Tzu and the commentators are pushing.

# Conclusion: Sun Tzu as practitioner of strategy from contrast

## Pulling the chapter together:
- **Mass as one huge lump** → Sun Tzu pushes **subdivision**, **cheng–chi alternation**, and treating **cheng and chi as something you can swap**, not lock in forever.
- **“Normal” vs “surprise” moves as two separate boxes** → Sun Tzu pushes **mix and reuse** so maneuvers don’t run out.
- **Look like what you are** → Sun Tzu pushes **feigned** weakness or strength depending on whether you are **stronger** or **weaker** — you steer what the enemy **thinks** they see.
- **Disorder = defeat** → Sun Tzu pushes **planned** movement that only **looks** chaotic, with **signals** and **set roles**.
- **Fire first, fire fast** → Sun Tzu pushes **restraint** until **range and moment** make the hit count.

### Why this matters for the assignment:
- Sun Tzu is doing **strategy from contrast** in a plain way: he keeps pointing **against** what people **assume** about size, appearances, neatness, and instant reaction.
- The job of the strategist, in this reading of the Energy chapter, is to notice where **the obvious story** about the fight **is wrong** — and to use **order in small pieces**, **deliberate signs**, and **timing** to exploit that gap.
